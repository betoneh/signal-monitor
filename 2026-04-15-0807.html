Today's Deep Dive: Schumpeterian Economics: Why 99% of AI Value Goes to Users

469w
Source: Marc Andreessen: Will a16z Go Public & Why Labour Displacement with AI is Wrong (20VC) · KB #13

Andreessen introduces a framework that should reshape how anyone thinks about AI investing: Schumpeterian economics and the concept of consumer surplus. Named after Joseph Schumpeter, the economist who developed creative destruction theory, this analysis shows that for every major technology platform in history, approximately 99% of the total economic value created accrues to users, not to the companies that build the technology.

The math is straightforward. Take the internet. The total economic value the internet has created — the productivity gains, new businesses enabled, connections made, information accessed, entertainment consumed — is astronomical, likely in the hundreds of trillions of dollars. The combined market capitalization of every internet company ever created is a tiny fraction of that. Amazon, Google, Meta, and every other internet company combined captured maybe 1% of the total value the internet produced. The other 99% went to the billions of people who use the internet to run their lives and businesses.

The same pattern holds for every prior technology platform. Who captured the value of electricity? Not General Electric — it was every factory, household, and business that used electricity to become more productive. Who captured the value of smartphones? Not Apple and Samsung — it was the billions of people whose lives were transformed by having a supercomputer in their pocket. The companies building the technology capture a sliver; users capture the ocean.

Andreessen argues AI will follow the identical pattern, and possibly even more extremely. The best AI in the world is already a consumer product — a $20/month app on your phone. Within a few years, 5 billion smartphone users will have access to AI that would have been science fiction a decade ago. Google is giving away enormous AI value for free. Open source models are increasingly competitive. The hyperdemocratization of AI is happening faster than any previous technology.

The investment implication is sobering: the AI companies investors are fighting to fund at astronomical valuations are competing for approximately 1% of the total value their technology creates. The other 99% is "dark matter" — distributed across billions of users, never tallied, never attributed to anyone, but constituting the overwhelming majority of AI's economic impact.

This reframes the "will AI companies justify their valuations" debate. The question isn't whether AI creates enough value — it clearly creates staggering value. The question is whether that value stays captured in the companies or leaks to users. History's answer is unambiguous: it leaks. Almost all of it. This doesn't mean AI companies can't be good investments — Amazon captured a tiny fraction of the internet's value and became one of the most valuable companies in history. But it means the ceiling for AI company value capture is much lower than the ceiling for AI value creation, and investors who confuse the two will get burned.
