Today's Deep Dive: Europe's Knowing-Doing Gap: The Draghi Report Problem

270w
Source: Marc Andreessen: Will a16z Go Public & Why Labour Displacement with AI is Wrong (20VC) · KB #13

Andreessen's description of his conversations with European heads of state is both charitable and devastating. Over 30 years, the conversation is "always the same": they explain they want a Silicon Valley equivalent, Andreessen lists the specific policy changes needed (labor flexibility, lower regulation, different tax structures), and they respond: "What if we can't do those things?"

The tragedy isn't ignorance — it's knowledge without action. Mario Draghi's 2024 report on European competitiveness diagnosed every problem and prescribed every solution. It's all in there. And "what's not happening is any of those things." European politicians, Andreessen observes, are often brilliant in private — "when you get them in private, they know everything" — but incapable of executing in public because the political costs of the necessary reforms exceed the political rewards.

This creates what you might call a knowing-doing gap: the collective intelligence of the system identifies the correct solutions, but the collective incentive structure of the system prevents their implementation. Everyone knows what needs to be done. Nobody can afford to do it. So they ask for "option B" — a painless alternative that doesn't exist.

Andreessen frames this as genuinely tragic, not merely dismissive. He's "tremendously pro-European" and "an Anglophile and a Francophile." He wishes it were different. But his pragmatic advice to his own firm is: "Back every single European founder who moves to the US." The move itself is the filter — it indicates both talent (European education systems are excellent) and willingness to seek risk (leaving comfort for uncertainty). This is a devastating indictment, made more devastating by being delivered with genuine affection rather than contempt.
