Today's Deep Dive: Physics Is Not About Decimal Places — It's About the Unseen

281w
Source: What Is Quantum Mechanics Really Telling Us? — David Deutsch & Brian Greene | World Science Festival · KB #26

David Deutsch draws a sharp line between physics as prediction and physics as explanation. The idea that physics exists to chase the sixth and seventh decimal place of experimental predictions is, in his view, absurd — nobody would dedicate a career to that alone. Physics, like the rest of science, is fundamentally about understanding what is actually going on in the world, particularly in the parts of reality we cannot directly observe.

This principle — that scientific explanation means explaining the seen in terms of the unseen — has deep consequences for how we evaluate quantum mechanics. If the purpose of a theory is merely to generate correct numbers, then any formalism that does so is equally good, and questions about what the theory says about reality become optional. But if the purpose is understanding, those questions become the entire point.

Deutsch argues that adopting mysticism or instrumentalism about quantum mechanics — treating the theory as a black box that outputs predictions — doesn't just leave questions unanswered; it actively prevents progress. A physicist trained not to ask what is really happening is a physicist who cannot discover the next theory. Only by demanding that quantum mechanics describe reality, not just measurement statistics, can we hope to identify the genuine problems whose solutions will lead beyond current physics.

This stance explains why Deutsch insists that so-called "interpretations" are actually rival theories with different ontologies and different claims about reality. Dismissing the differences as mere philosophical gloss is, in his view, a failure of intellectual seriousness — one that traces back to the cultural influence of Niels Bohr and the Copenhagen tradition, which trained generations of physicists to substitute abstractions for realism.
