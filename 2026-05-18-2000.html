Today's Deep Dive: Agency Over Outcomes: The Real Metric Elite Founders Optimize For

249w
Source: David Senra's Conversation with Marc Andreessen (@pmarca) – Founders, Tech as Progress, and the Zero Introspection Mindset · KB #25

One of the most striking ideas in Marc Andreessen's conversation is the suggestion that the best founders aren't optimizing for conventional success metrics—revenue, exits, status—but for something harder to quantify: agency. The ability to shape reality, to bend the world toward a vision, is what actually sustains founders through the brutal stretches where happiness is nowhere to be found.

This reframing has significant implications for how we evaluate founder motivation. The standard narrative assumes founders are driven by some combination of wealth and recognition. But Andreessen's framing suggests those are byproducts, not drivers. The core compulsion is closer to what philosophers might call will to power—not in a domineering sense, but as the deep human need to act on the world and see the results of that action.

This explains why many successful founders don't stop after their first win. If the goal were money or comfort, rational behavior would dictate retirement after a significant exit. Instead, serial founders keep building because the thing they're actually addicted to—agency, impact, the feeling of making a dent—doesn't have a satiation point. It's an appetite that grows with feeding.

For investors and team members, understanding this distinction is critical. Founders driven primarily by agency will make decisions that look irrational through a pure financial lens—turning down acquisitions, pursuing ambitious pivots, or doubling down on technical bets with uncertain payoffs. Recognizing that these decisions are coherent within an agency-maximizing framework helps everyone around the founder operate with better alignment and fewer misunderstandings.
