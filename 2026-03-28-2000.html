Today's Deep Dive: The Selfish Bit: Verdon's Update to Dawkins

426w
Source: Vitalik Buterin vs Beff Jezos/Guillaume Verdon debate · KB #11

Richard Dawkins' "selfish gene" reframed evolution: genes don't serve organisms; organisms serve genes. Genes are the unit of selection, and they "want" (metaphorically) to replicate. Guillaume Verdon proposes an even more fundamental unit: the selfish bit.

In Dawkins' framework, the replicator is biological — DNA sequences. But Verdon argues that the true unit of selection is any bit of information that prescribes a configuration of matter: a gene, a meme, a chemical formula, a product design, a policy, a cultural norm. These bits exist in a vast competitive landscape where they're constantly being tested against reality. Bits that help the systems they're part of persist and grow get replicated. Bits that don't get discarded.

This is a genuinely novel synthesis. It unifies biological evolution, cultural evolution, and technological evolution under a single framework. A gene that codes for a more efficient protein is selected for the same reason a business model that captures more market share is selected for: both help their host system capture more energy and persist longer. The selection mechanism is different (natural selection vs. market competition vs. memetic spread), but the underlying thermodynamic logic is identical.

The implications are profound. If bits are the unit of selection, then what matters isn't the substrate — carbon or silicon — but whether the information is useful for growth and persistence. This means AI models aren't competitors to biological life; they're just another substrate for information to inhabit. The bits don't care whether they're encoded in DNA, neural weights, or silicon transistors. They "care" about replicating.

This reframing dissolves some common anxieties about AI. If you're worried about AI "replacing" humans, the selfish bit framework says the question is wrong. The real question is: which bits of information will persist? If human-generated knowledge, values, and cultural patterns are useful for the growth of the overall system, they'll persist — embedded in AI systems, hybrid human-AI organisms, or whatever comes next. If they're not useful, they won't, regardless of whether AI exists.

The darker implication: human values might not be "useful" from a thermodynamic perspective. Compassion, art, leisure — these could be selected against if they reduce a system's energy capture rate. Verdon would argue that variance and exploration (including seemingly "useless" activities) are themselves selected for because they enable better search over design space. But this is an empirical bet, not a guarantee.

The selfish bit is Dawkins for the information age — elegant, provocative, and uncomfortably reductive. Like the selfish gene before it, its power lies not in being complete, but in being clarifying.
