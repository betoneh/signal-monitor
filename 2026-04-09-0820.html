Today's Deep Dive: Ephemeralization: Buckminster Fuller's Vision of Doing More With Less

598w
Source: The Techno-Optimist Manifesto — Marc Andreessen · KB #2

Buckminster Fuller coined the word "ephemeralization" in 1938 to describe a trend he observed across the history of technology: doing more and more with less and less material, until eventually you can do everything with essentially nothing. It's an ungainly word for a profound idea — one that economists now call dematerialization — and it has become one of the most empirically well-supported claims in the techno-optimist canon.

Fuller was an architect, engineer, futurist, and professional eccentric who spent his life designing systems at the intersection of physics and human flourishing. His most famous invention, the geodesic dome, is itself an embodiment of ephemeralization: a structure that achieves extraordinary strength-to-weight ratios by distributing stress across its entire surface rather than concentrating it in pillars. The same enclosed volume, vastly less material. The same structural function, achieved through geometry rather than brute mass.

The historical pattern Fuller identified is striking. The first transatlantic telegraph cables required enormous amounts of copper and were fragile and expensive. Each subsequent generation of communication technology carried more information using fewer physical resources. Today a fiber-optic cable the width of a human hair carries millions of times more information than those early copper cables, using photons rather than electrons, at a tiny fraction of the energy cost. The trend across 150 years is unmistakable: more signal, less stuff.

The same pattern holds across industries. A Boeing 787 uses 20% less fuel than the aircraft it replaced, with a higher percentage of its structure made from carbon fiber composites rather than aluminum. A modern smartphone contains more computing power than a room-sized mainframe of the 1970s, at a millionth of the energy cost. Global steel consumption per unit of GDP has been falling for decades as structures are engineered more efficiently. LED lighting delivers the same lumens as incandescent bulbs using 80% less electricity. The physical economy is quietly becoming lighter, even as it becomes richer.

The economist Jesse Ausubel and his colleagues at Rockefeller University have documented this dematerialization across dozens of industries and materials — a body of research that directly challenges the resource-depletion narrative. The US economy uses less steel, aluminum, copper, and timber per unit of economic output than it did in 1970, and in absolute terms many of these have peaked as well. The idea that economic growth necessarily means proportionally more resource consumption is empirically false, and has been for decades.

Fuller's broader framework connected ephemeralization to a long-run vision of universal abundance. If you can do the same work with less material each generation, then the long-run trajectory of technology is toward a world where the physical constraints on human flourishing — scarcity of raw materials, energy inputs, physical labor — become progressively less binding. Andreessen's version of this is that physical goods should eventually become "as cheap as pencils" — not because pencils are worthless, but because they are produced so efficiently that their material scarcity is irrelevant to your decision to use them.

The interesting tension in Fuller's vision is between ephemeralization and energy. Doing more with less material typically requires more sophisticated information processing — better design, smarter algorithms, more complex manufacturing. Information processing requires energy. The dematerialization of physical goods is partly offset by the increased energy intensity of the knowledge economy that produces them. Fuller anticipated this: his answer was the same technology curve applied to energy — solar, nuclear, eventually fusion — that would make energy itself effectively free. At that point, the material limits on human ambition dissolve into pure physics. Fuller called this "livingry" — the technology of life support — and he thought it was the actual project of civilization, if we were serious about it.
