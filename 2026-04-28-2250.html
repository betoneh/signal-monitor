Today's Deep Dive: The Environmental Precedent: Why Version N+1 Fixes Version N (But Not Automatically)

610w
Source: My Techno-Optimism — Vitalik Buterin · KB #14

One of the most powerful arguments for techno-optimism rests on a pattern that repeats throughout history: the same civilization that created a problem eventually develops the technology to solve it. Industrial Britain gave us the Great Smog of 1952 and cities where ambient pollution reduced life expectancy by years. That same industrial society eventually produced the Clean Air Acts, catalytic converters, and a century of ongoing improvement in air quality. Today, in many cities, air quality is better than it was before industrialization began. The forest area of France is larger today than it was in 1800. The ozone layer is recovering. Acid rain is improving.

Vitalik Buterin uses this pattern to make a pointed argument against the anti-technology instinct: if version N of civilization's technology causes a problem, the answer is version N+1, not going back to version N-1. This is empirically correct across a wide range of environmental challenges. The Green Revolution caused problems with pesticides and monoculture — and also fed billions of people who would otherwise have starved. The same agricultural science that created those problems is now producing drought-resistant varieties, precision fermentation, and cultured meat that could eliminate the largest ongoing moral catastrophe in human history.

But Buterin's key move is the critical qualifier: this doesn't happen automatically. The ozone layer is recovering because the Montreal Protocol mandated it. Air pollution improved because governments set standards and companies were legally required to meet them. Solar power got cheap not through the magic of markets alone but through decades of deliberate public and private investment motivated by decades of advocacy about climate change. Behind every environmental success story is intentional coordinated human action — public discourse shaping policy, scientists advocating, philanthropists funding, governments mandating.

This is Buterin's core critique of the pure accelerationist position, and it's a sharp one. The e/acc narrative attributes these successes to the "techno-capital machine" — the inexorable logic of markets finding solutions. But the historical record doesn't support this. Markets alone did not ban CFCs. Markets alone did not build the interstate highway system's emissions standards. The techno-capital machine, left to its own devices, optimizes for profit, not for the kinds of externalities that pollution, pandemic risk, and climate change represent. The clean version of the technology only arrives when humans intentionally choose to build it and create conditions that reward doing so.

The implication for AI and biotech is direct. Yes, AI will probably eventually produce systems that help address the problems AI created — that's the N+1 pattern. But "eventually" could mean after a catastrophic lock-in event. The environmental analogy has a dark edge: we spent decades producing CFCs before we fixed the ozone layer, and that particular fix was relatively easy. Some environmental problems — extinctions, certain forms of ecosystem collapse — are not recoverable on human timescales. The question for AI and biotech is which problems are ozone-layer-fixable and which are extinction-permanent. That uncertainty is precisely why Buterin argues for intentionality rather than letting the techno-capital machine pick winners. The N+1 solution is more likely to arrive in time if we explicitly try to build it, rather than waiting for market incentives to accidentally select for it.
