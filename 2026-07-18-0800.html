Today's Deep Dive: D/ACC: Vitalik's Defensive Acceleration

442w
Source: Vitalik Buterin vs Beff Jezos/Guillaume Verdon debate · KB #11

Vitalik Buterin coined "d/acc" — defensive acceleration — as a deliberate counterpoint to e/acc. It's often misunderstood as deceleration or caution. It's neither. It's a specific strategy: accelerate the hell out of defensive technologies while being deliberate about the pace of offensive capabilities.

The core insight is that not all technologies are created equal in terms of their effect on power dynamics. Some technologies are "offense-dominant" — they make it easier to attack, control, or concentrate power. Surveillance AI, autonomous weapons, and centralized compute clusters fall into this category. Other technologies are "defense-dominant" — they make it easier to protect, resist, and distribute power. Encryption, open source software, decentralized networks, bio-safety infrastructure, and privacy-preserving hardware fall here.

Vitalik's argument is that we should be racing to develop defensive technologies precisely because offensive capabilities are advancing so fast. If AGI arrives and the world hasn't built robust defenses — privacy infrastructure, verifiable hardware, biosecurity, decentralized governance — then whoever controls AGI controls everything. Permanently. This is what he calls "unipolar risk": the scenario where advanced technology enables a single entity (government, corporation, or even an AI itself) to establish permanent, inescapable control.

The concrete projects he's funded illustrate this: air quality monitors that use fully homomorphic encryption to collect public health data without exposing individual privacy. Open-source biosecurity tools that could give any country COVID-level pandemic resistance with Sweden-level freedom. Verifiable hardware that can cryptographically prove what a camera is and isn't recording.

D/acc explicitly rejects the framing that you must choose between progress and safety. Instead, it says: the fastest possible progress on defense is the thing that makes offense-progress safe. It's not about slowing down the train; it's about building better brakes and crash barriers while the train accelerates.

The strategic nuance is in the 4-year argument. Vitalik doesn't want to delay AI forever — he argues that 4 extra years before AGI (say, arriving in 2032 instead of 2028) would be enormously valuable because it gives humanity time to deploy defensive infrastructure. And critically, 4 years starting in 2028 are worth "100 times more" than 4 years in the 1960s, because we now have the tools to actually solve alignment and build the defenses.

Critics (including Verdon) argue this is geopolitically naive — you can't unilaterally slow down a global technology race. Vitalik counters that the chip supply chain is already one of the most centralized things on Earth (Taiwan produces 70%+ of all advanced chips), so throttling hardware is more feasible than it appears.

D/acc is the engineer's response to the philosopher's dilemma: don't argue about whether to accelerate. Just make sure the shields are stronger than the swords.
