Today's Deep Dive: Silicon Valley's Political Shift Was a Preference Cascade Hiding in Plain Sight

301w
Source: Tariffs, DOGE & Fixing America: Peter Thiel's Skeptic Solutions · KB #33

For years, Silicon Valley presented a monolithically center-left political front. Peter Thiel, who served on Meta's board for 17 years from 2005 to 2022, identifies three competing explanations: founders like Zuckerberg were genuinely left-wing, a woke millennial workforce created bottom-up pressure, or a left-wing regulatory state exerted top-down coercion. Thiel believes the third factor was the most powerful — the enormous surface area where tech companies could be regulated created constant incentives to appease progressive demands.

The dynamic followed a predictable escalation pattern. Companies would make incremental concessions — implementing DEI programs, adjusting content moderation policies — believing these were moderate accommodations that would satisfy regulators and activist employees. When the concessions didn't produce the promised results, the response was always to do more, never to reverse course. Founders who weren't deeply political simply wanted to build products, not spend time at congressional hearings, so appeasement was the path of least resistance.

Behind closed doors, however, the gap between private beliefs and public statements among tech CEOs grew enormous. Thiel describes conversations where leaders of major companies would share views with him that were radically different from their public positions. One large tech company's internal strategy was to quietly move employees out of San Francisco each year to reduce the influence of its most ideologically driven workforce — a plan they could never discuss publicly.

Thiel connects this to economist Timur Kuran's theory of preference falsification: when everybody is lying and everybody knows that everybody is lying, the system becomes ripe for a sudden cascade. Once enough people recognized the universal dishonesty, the entire edifice collapsed rapidly. Speech codes and restrictions on free expression ultimately backfire on their proponents because they don't cause people to flip at the margins — they set up conditions for a radical, sudden reversal.
