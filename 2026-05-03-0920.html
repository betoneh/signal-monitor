Today's Deep Dive: The Anti-Industrial Revolution: Rand's Warning About Safety Culture

470w
Source: The New Left: The Anti-Industrial Revolution — Ayn Rand / Atlas Shrugged — Ayn Rand

In 1971, Rand published an essay called "The Anti-Industrial Revolution" — a polemic against the emerging environmentalist movement. Her argument wasn't that pollution is fine. It was that the movement's real target wasn't pollution but industrialization itself — the act of humans reshaping nature through technology.

Fifty-five years later, the pattern she identified has metastasized into what we now call safety culture — and it's no longer just about factories and rivers. It's about AI alignment, crypto regulation, tech antitrust, biosafety, nuclear energy, and every domain where human capability butts against institutional fear.

The d/acc vs e/acc debate is the Anti-Industrial Revolution, updated. Vitalik's defensive accelerationism argues for building with guardrails. Beff Jezos and the e/acc movement argue that guardrails become cages. Both are right about different things, and both are wrong about the same thing: they treat safety and acceleration as a spectrum when Rand and Nietzsche saw them as a binary.

The binary isn't "safe vs dangerous." It's "who decides." When the individual builder decides what risks to take — Rearden testing his own metal, a DeFi developer deploying on mainnet, a founder launching without regulatory blessing — the risk is owned by the risk-taker. When the committee decides, the risk is "managed" into nonexistence, and so is the innovation. Directive 10-289 doesn't mention safety once. It doesn't need to. Freezing everything in place is the ultimate safety measure.

Multi-agent reliability math illustrates this perfectly: 10 agents at 90% individual accuracy produce 35% system reliability. The safety-culture response is to demand 99.9% accuracy before deployment. The builder response is to build better orchestration — the same way aviation doesn't make perfect engines but makes systems that handle engine failure. The Last Man wants to eliminate failure. The Übermensch wants to survive it productively.

Karpathy's AI job exposure treemap — every US occupation scored for AI displacement risk, average 5.3/10 — is the Anti-Industrial Revolution's nightmare made data. Half the workforce's cognitive labor is automatable. The safety response: slow down, regulate, protect jobs. Rand's response: the jobs that machines can do were never human work to begin with. Human work is the thing machines can't do yet — judgment, taste, the decision of what's worth building.

The Anti-Industrial Revolution keeps returning because the Last Man keeps returning. His blink is the same whether he's protesting nuclear power in 1971, opposing crypto in 2022, or demanding AI safety theater in 2026. He doesn't want safety. He wants stillness. And stillness, Nietzsche knew, is just death with a longer timeline.
