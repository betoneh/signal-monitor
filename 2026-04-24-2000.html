Today's Deep Dive: Price Deflation as Wealth Creation: The Economics Nobody Teaches

638w
Source: Marc Andreessen: The Real AI Boom Hasn't Even Started Yet — Lenny's Podcast · KB #5

There is a version of economics taught in almost every school, reflected in almost every newspaper, and embedded in almost every policy framework, in which falling prices are a symptom of crisis. Deflation is the word that haunts central bankers. The logic is coherent: if consumers expect prices to fall, they delay purchases, demand collapses, firms cut production, workers are laid off, and the cycle feeds on itself. Japan's lost decade. The Great Depression. The mechanism is real. The problem is that it describes one kind of deflation while ignoring another kind entirely — and the second kind is responsible for most of the actual improvement in human living standards over the past two centuries.

Marc Andreessen, in conversation with Lenny Rachitsky, returns to this distinction as one of his core frameworks for understanding what technology actually does to an economy. The deflation that destroys demand is demand-side deflation: falling prices caused by collapsing consumption, excess capacity, and debt spirals. The deflation that technology produces is supply-side deflation: falling prices caused by genuine productivity gains, where more output is produced per unit of input. These are not the same phenomenon. They have opposite causes and opposite consequences.

The canonical example is computing. In 1965, a megabyte of storage cost approximately $1 million. Today it costs a fraction of a cent. This is not demand destruction. Nobody stopped wanting storage. The collapse in price was driven entirely by supply-side improvement — better lithography, better materials science, better manufacturing processes — and it did not cause a deflationary spiral. It caused an explosion in the number of applications that became economically viable. Every time the price of a capability drops, the population of problems it can be applied to expands. The cheaper the tool, the wider the use case.

This is the dynamic Andreessen argues AI is now reproducing at scale, and why he is unbothered by the prospect of AI driving down the cost of cognitive labor. The standard fear is that cheaper cognition means fewer jobs, lower wages, and economic stagnation. The historical analog suggests the opposite. Cheaper cognition means that the population of problems humans can afford to apply cognitive resources to expands dramatically. Problems that were previously uneconomic — too labor-intensive, too specialized, too diffuse — become tractable. New categories of demand appear that did not exist when cognition was expensive.

William Nordhaus, the Yale economist who won the Nobel Prize for integrating climate into macroeconomic modeling, produced a striking calculation: when measured in terms of the price of light per lumen-hour, the cost of artificial illumination has fallen by a factor of roughly 500,000 since the pre-industrial era. By conventional GDP accounting, almost none of this improvement is captured — because we spend less on lighting, not more, and GDP measures spending. The entire productivity gain from cheap light essentially disappears from the official record. Andreessen's broader argument is that this accounting failure is not a rounding error. It is a systematic bias that causes economists, journalists, and policymakers to consistently underestimate how much better things are getting.

The implication for AI is that standard economic models will, once again, miss most of the value. If AI reduces the cost of medical diagnosis, legal research, software development, and tutoring by an order of magnitude, GDP will register whatever portion of that reduction flows through market transactions. The vast majority — the problems that get solved only because they are now affordable, the questions that get asked only because the answer is cheap — will be invisible to the official statistics. This is not an argument that AI will solve everything. It is an argument that the accounting system we use to measure progress is poorly designed to capture supply-side technological deflation, and that we should expect to be systematically surprised on the upside.
