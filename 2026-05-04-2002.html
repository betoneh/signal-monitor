Today's Deep Dive: Regulate Uses, Not Math: The Only Coherent AI Policy

580w
Source: On Tech Politics/Policy — Marc Andreessen & Ben Horowitz (a16z, April 2024) · KB #15

The cleanest test for any AI regulation proposal is also the simplest: does it regulate a use, or does it regulate the math? Andreessen and Horowitz frame this as the binary that everything else collapses into. Regulating uses is sane, enforceable, and consistent with how every other technology in history has been governed. Regulating the math is incoherent, unenforceable, and a guaranteed monopoly maintenance program.

Andreessen's strongest version of the argument runs through a simple observation: every harmful use of AI is already illegal under existing law. AI used to discriminate in lending? Already illegal under fair lending statutes that go back decades. AI used to plan a crime? Already illegal under conspiracy and accessory law. AI used to generate CSAM? Already illegal under child protection law. AI used for election fraud? Already illegal under election law. The case law, the enforcement infrastructure, and the prosecutorial expertise all already exist. There is no use of AI to do something bad that current law fails to cover.

The Doomer activist response to this is to insist that AI is somehow categorically different — that the technology itself, prior to any specific use, requires preemptive constraint. Andreessen and Horowitz dismantle this by reducing it to its actual content: the proposal is to regulate linear algebra above N variables. AI models, stripped of mystique, are equations with billions of parameters. The dangerous version of "regulate the technology" is a regulation that says: this many variables is fine, this many is forbidden. There is no intellectually honest way to draw that line — and the people drawing it know it.

The nuclear power analogy is the one that lands hardest. We don't regulate physics. We regulate uranium enrichment, weapons proliferation, reactor safety, and waste disposal. You can study quantum mechanics in any university in the world. You cannot build a centrifuge cascade in your garage. The regulatory layer applies to downstream actions with real-world consequences, not to the underlying knowledge or computation. Imagine instead a world where, post-Hiroshima, we'd banned the study of physics because nuclear weapons exist. We'd be living in mud huts, and the countries that ignored the ban would have all the bombs.

This matters because the regulate-the-math camp has a very specific commercial outcome they want: only three or four companies allowed to do AI. If you ban open source, ban small models, require pre-deployment licensing, and impose compute limits, the only entities that can comply are Microsoft, Google, Amazon-via-Anthropic, and maybe Meta. Everything else is illegal by construction. The startup ecosystem dies. The university research pipeline dies. The competitive pricing pressure that's currently driving API costs down dies. And the global open-source community moves to jurisdictions that didn't pass the law — which means the math still gets done, just not in America.

The use-based regulation alternative is the one already proven to work. If an AI is used for a financial crime, prosecute the financial crime. If it's used for harassment, prosecute the harassment. If it generates illegal content, prosecute under the existing illegal content statutes. The technology becomes a tool, like every other tool, and the legal system focuses on the harm rather than the implement. This is the framework that has governed every previous wave of dual-use technology — print, photography, encryption, the internet itself — and it's the framework that has consistently produced both innovation and reasonable safety outcomes.

Anyone proposing AI regulation that does not pass the use-vs-math test should be assumed to be either (a) confused about the technology, or (b) lobbying for a specific commercial outcome. There is no third option.
