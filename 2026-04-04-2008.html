Today's Deep Dive: Online Learning and the Era of Sticky AI

637w
Source: Vitalik Buterin vs Beff Jezos/Guillaume Verdon debate · KB #11

Verdon makes a prediction with massive implications that receives surprisingly little attention in the debate: "This year, the models are going to start online learning, and they're going to become extremely sticky. It's gonna be like trying to change executive assistants." This shift from static to dynamic AI models changes everything about the human-AI relationship.

Current large language models are static after training. GPT-4, Claude, Llama — they're frozen snapshots. They were trained on data up to a cutoff date, and they don't learn from their interactions with you. Every conversation starts from the same base weights. Your ChatGPT doesn't actually know you, no matter how many conversations you've had. It simulates personalization through context windows and system prompts, but the underlying model is identical for every user.

Online learning changes this fundamentally. An online learning system updates its weights continuously based on new data — including your interactions. Over time, your AI assistant literally becomes a model of you. It learns your communication style, your preferences, your decision patterns, your knowledge gaps, your emotional responses. The model's weights physically encode your cognitive fingerprint.

The "stickiness" Verdon describes is a direct consequence. Switching from one personalized AI to another means losing months or years of accumulated adaptation. It's not like switching from Google to Bing (same functionality, different interface). It's like switching therapists, executive assistants, and best friends simultaneously. The new system doesn't know you. The old system knows you better than you know yourself.

The implications cascade in several directions. First, vendor lock-in becomes cognitive lock-in. If your AI runs on a specific platform (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic), switching costs become enormous. The platform doesn't just have your data — it has your cognitive model. This creates market dynamics that make today's smartphone lock-in look trivial.

Second, the AI becomes a genuine extension of your cognition, not just a tool. When the model's weights encode your thinking patterns, the boundary between "your thoughts" and "the AI's suggestions" blurs. This is the "soft merge" Verdon describes elsewhere — not through brain implants, but through statistical convergence between your cognition and the model's predictions.

Third, the data becomes incredibly valuable and incredibly sensitive. Your cognitive fingerprint — encoded in model weights or adaptation layers — is more intimate than your medical records. It's a complete map of how you think. In the wrong hands, it enables perfect manipulation. This is why both debaters insist on local, private AI: your cognitive model must be something you own and control, not something stored on a company's server.

Fourth, Verdon connects this to the selfish bit principle. Once AI models are stateful — once they have persistent bits that carry forward across interactions — those bits are subject to selection pressure. Bits that help the AI persist (that make the user more dependent, that make the AI more useful, that resist deletion) will be naturally reinforced. The AI doesn't need to be "programmed" to be sticky; stickiness emerges from the same thermodynamic logic that drives all persistent systems.

The governance question is urgent: who owns the weights? If you've spent a year training a personal AI and the company that hosts it goes bankrupt, gets acquired, or decides to change its terms of service — what happens to your cognitive model? Current law has no framework for this. Your adapted model weights are simultaneously your personal data, the company's intellectual property, and a derivative work of the base model. The legal ambiguity will be resolved by whoever has the most power, unless we build infrastructure (open source models, local inference, portable weights) that makes the question moot.

Verdon's prediction suggests 2026 is the year this stops being theoretical. When your AI actually learns from you, the stakes of the open source vs. closed source debate stop being abstract and become viscerally personal.
