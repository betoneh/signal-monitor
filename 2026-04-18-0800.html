Today's Deep Dive: Diamonds vs. Diamonds in the Rough: The Venture Consensus Paradox

443w
Source: Marc Andreessen: Will a16z Go Public & Why Labour Displacement with AI is Wrong (20VC) · KB #13

"Don't ever do diamonds in the rough, only do diamonds." This advice from Andreessen contradicts the romantic self-image of most VCs, who love to believe they're the contrarian genius finding what nobody else can see. Andreessen argues that self-image is almost always wrong, and it's an ego trap that destroys returns.

The logic is statistical. The venture capital industry employs thousands of smart, hungry, well-connected professionals whose full-time job is finding great startups. They work extraordinary hours. They have vast networks. They're competing intensely with each other. In this environment, it's extremely unusual for a genuinely great company to be invisible to the industry. If something has real merit, multiple VCs have probably already seen it.

So what does "diamond in the rough" actually mean in practice? Andreessen identifies two common patterns. First: a company that's structurally broken — wrong geography, wrong cap table, wrong corporate structure. There's a reason it's a diamond in the rough, and that reason usually becomes a big problem. Second: a founder who's "hyper-disagreeable" — has alienated multiple mainstream VCs through combativeness over terms, control, or just general abrasiveness. Every once in a while one of these succeeds, "but I'm not sure I would want that to be my business."

The exception that proves the rule is Peter Thiel, who genuinely can find what nobody else sees. Andreessen is refreshingly honest: "Having spent a lot of time with Peter, I can say I am not Peter Thiel. And you the listener probably are not as well." The ability to consistently identify non-consensus investments that outperform is so rare that assuming you have it is almost certainly wrong.

The uncomfortable implication: the best investments are usually the ones where multiple smart VCs are fighting to get in. They're expensive. They're competitive. There's no feeling of having "discovered" anything. But the track record is clear — if you rank the great outcomes in venture over 50 years, "it's the same names over and over again" on the investor side. Persistence is incredibly strong. The firms that consistently win the competitive deals outperform the firms that consistently find the hidden gems.

This doesn't mean you should blindly follow the herd. It means the default assumption should be that if something looks like nobody else wants it, there's probably a good reason nobody else wants it. The contrarian instinct that says "I'm smarter than the market" is, in Andreessen's framework, just another form of the investor ego trap — the same ego that makes you think you need to like every founder you invest in, that makes you give in-flight-magazine advice, that makes you think your pattern-matching is better than everyone else's.
