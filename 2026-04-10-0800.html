Today's Deep Dive: Julian Simon's Ultimate Resource: Why More People Means More Progress

618w
Source: The Techno-Optimist Manifesto — Marc Andreessen · KB #2

In 1980, economist Julian Simon issued a public wager to Paul Ehrlich — the same biologist who had predicted mass starvation in the 1970s. Simon bet that the real prices of five commodities Ehrlich could choose would fall over the next decade, not rise. Ehrlich, confident in his resource-depletion thesis, chose copper, chromium, nickel, tin, and tungsten. By 1990, all five had declined in real price. Ehrlich mailed Simon a check for $576.07 without comment. The bet has become one of the most cited empirical victories in the history of economics.

The wager encapsulates Simon's central argument, developed at book length in The Ultimate Resource (1981): human ingenuity is the ultimate resource, and more people means more ingenuity. Resource scarcity, in Simon's framework, is a solvable problem — and the solver is the same thing that makes scarcity look alarming in the first place: a growing population of creative, problem-solving human beings. Population growth is not the cause of resource depletion; it is the engine of resource creation through technological innovation.

Simon's argument inverts the standard Malthusian logic in a specific way. Malthus treated agricultural technology as roughly fixed and asked: how many mouths can the existing land feed? Simon treats technology as the key variable and asks: how many problems can a population of curious humans solve? The answer, empirically, is always: more problems than they create. Every generation inherits not just the problems of the previous generation but also the solutions — and the solutions compound faster than the problems because they are recorded and transmitted rather than forgotten.

The mechanism runs through prices. When a resource becomes scarce, its price rises. Rising price signals to entrepreneurs that there's profit in finding alternatives, improving extraction, or reducing demand through efficiency. If the price stays high, someone finds a solution — a new deposit, a substitute material, a more efficient process — and the price falls. Simon documented this pattern across hundreds of commodities over decades. Oil, copper, food, timber — in the long run, the price of almost every natural resource falls in real terms, adjusted for wages, because the technology for extracting and using it improves faster than demand grows.

Simon's case for population growth is more counterintuitive and requires more care. He is not arguing that more people is trivially good in all circumstances — he acknowledged that rapid population growth can overwhelm existing institutions and infrastructure in the short run. His claim is about the long-run: a larger population means a larger pool of potential innovators, scientists, entrepreneurs, and problem-solvers. The person who will cure cancer, design the next energy breakthrough, or develop the crop variety that feeds the next billion people has to be born first. Population caps, in this logic, are caps on the number of potential Borlaugs.

This argument puts Simon in direct conflict with the Club of Rome's 1972 report The Limits to Growth, which used computer modeling to argue that exponential resource consumption on a finite planet must eventually hit hard limits. Simon's response was methodological: the models treated technology as exogenous (a fixed factor) rather than endogenous (a response to scarcity). When you build human problem-solving into the model, the predicted resource crises don't materialize — because they are precisely the kind of visible, high-stakes, price-signaled problems that markets and scientists are best at solving.

The modern extension of Simon's argument applies directly to AI. If human minds are the ultimate resource, and AI can amplify the effective output of each human mind — not just in productivity but in the capacity to generate new ideas — then the arrival of artificial intelligence is the most dramatic increase in the "ultimate resource" in human history. Every researcher with a capable AI assistant is, in Simon's terms, a more powerful problem-solver. The curve bends upward again.
