The Shattered Beliefs Cycle: From 1914 to 2026

3 min read · 586 words · Deep Dive #24 · 2026-03-28
Source: Vitalik Buterin vs Beff Jezos/Guillaume Verdon debate · KB #11

Vitalik opens his first extended response with a sweeping historical narrative: the cycle of techno-optimism, catastrophic disillusionment, and ideological reconstruction that has repeated throughout the last century. It's not just context-setting — it's an argument about where we are in the cycle right now.

The first wave: pre-World War I, roughly 1880–1914. This was the original age of techno-optimism. Electricity, chemistry, telecommunications, automobiles, aviation — all arriving in rapid succession. Living standards skyrocketed. Life expectancy increased dramatically. Women were being liberated from household drudgery by labor-saving machines. The prevailing belief was that technology would solve everything — poverty, disease, conflict. The 1900 World's Fair in Paris was a monument to this faith.

Then World War I arrived. "People rode in with horses and rode out on tanks," as Vitalik puts it. The same chemistry that produced fertilizers produced poison gas. The same engineering that built bridges built trenches. Technology didn't prevent war — it industrialized slaughter. The optimism of 1900 was obliterated in the mud of Verdun.

The second wave: post-WWII, roughly 1945–1970. Nuclear energy promised unlimited power. Space exploration promised new frontiers. The Green Revolution promised the end of famine. But this wave produced its own horrors: Hiroshima, the arms race, Vietnam, environmental destruction. "I am become death, destroyer of worlds" — Oppenheimer's words haunted an entire generation. The response was the counterculture, environmentalism, postmodernism — all attempts to make sense of technology's dual nature.

The third wave: the digital revolution, roughly 1990–2020. The internet would democratize information. Social media would connect humanity. Smartphones would empower the individual. And they did — but they also enabled mass surveillance, algorithmic manipulation, political polarization, and attention hijacking. The optimism of the early internet gave way to the techlash of the late 2010s.

Vitalik's most cutting example is what he calls "hipster environmentalism" — the belief that protecting nature meant opposing nuclear power. Germany shut down its nuclear plants, which made it dependent on Russian natural gas, which meant "your country is stuck bootlicking Russia." The beautiful belief system produced ugly geopolitical consequences. This is the pattern: idealistic responses to the previous cycle's failures create the conditions for the next cycle's crisis.

Now we're entering the fourth wave: AI and synthetic biology. The optimism is palpable — AI will cure diseases, eliminate drudgery, unlock human potential. E/acc is, in some sense, the latest expression of the perennial techno-optimist faith. D/acc is the attempt to learn from history: to maintain the optimism while engineering against the catastrophe.

The question this historical lens raises is uncomfortable for both sides. Techno-optimists have been wrong at every previous turn — not about the technology's potential, but about humanity's ability to deploy it wisely. The pre-WWI optimists were right that chemistry and electricity would transform the world; they were wrong that the transformation would be uniformly positive. Are today's AI optimists making the same mistake?

But the techno-skeptics have also been consistently wrong — not about the risks, but about the solution. Every attempt to slow or stop technological progress has either failed (nuclear proliferation wasn't prevented) or backfired (Germany's nuclear phase-out). The luddites were right that industrial machinery would destroy their way of life; they were wrong that smashing the machines would help.

Vitalik's implicit argument is that d/acc breaks this cycle by being neither naive optimism nor futile resistance. It's engineering applied to the problem of engineering's consequences. Whether it actually breaks the cycle or is just this generation's version of the same pattern — that's the question history will answer.
