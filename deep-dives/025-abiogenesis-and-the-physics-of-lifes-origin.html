Abiogenesis and the Physics of Life's Origin

When Verdon explains the intellectual origins of e/acc, he doesn't start with economics or politics — he starts with Abiogenesis" target="_blank">abiogenesis, the emergence of life from non-living matter. This isn't metaphor. He literally claims that understanding how life bootstrapped itself from "a soup of inorganic matter" is the key to understanding civilization, technology, and the future.

Abiogenesis remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in science. We know that approximately 3.8 billion years ago, chemical systems on early Earth crossed a threshold from complex chemistry to biology. But how? The gap between the most complex non-living chemistry and the simplest living cell is enormous. A living cell contains DNA (information storage), RNA (information transfer), proteins (molecular machines), membranes (containment), and metabolic pathways (energy processing) — all interdependent, all seemingly requiring each other to function.

The leading theories fall into several camps. "RNA World" proposes that RNA came first, serving as both information carrier and catalyst, with DNA and proteins evolving later. "Metabolism First" proposes that self-sustaining chemical cycles (proto-metabolisms) preceded genetic information. "Lipid World" proposes that self-assembling membranes created compartments where complex chemistry could concentrate. Each has evidence; none is complete.

Verdon's framework draws from a more fundamental perspective: thermodynamic theories of life's origin, particularly the work of Jeremy England at MIT. England's dissipation-driven adaptation theory proposes that matter, when subjected to energy flows (like sunlight hitting a warm pool), tends to organize in ways that increase entropy production. Not because it "wants" to — because configurations that dissipate more energy are statistically more stable in out-of-equilibrium conditions.

This is the bridge between physics and biology. Life doesn't emerge despite the second law of thermodynamics — it emerges because of it. A self-replicating molecule is better at dissipating energy than a non-replicating one, because it creates copies of itself that also dissipate energy. Natural selection is, in this view, a consequence of thermodynamics: the structures that dissipate energy most effectively (by being better adapted) are the ones that persist.

Verdon extrapolates this all the way up. If the same thermodynamic forces that created life from chemistry also drive the evolution of technology from biology, then civilization is just the latest phase of a 3.8-billion-year process. Transistors are to neurons what neurons were to RNA: a more efficient substrate for information processing and energy dissipation. AI is to human intelligence what human intelligence was to animal cognition: a phase transition to higher complexity.

This framework has a beautiful explanatory power and a terrifying implication. The beauty: it unifies physics, biology, and technology under a single set of equations. The terror: if the process is truly thermodynamic and Darwinian, then human values, human preferences, and human survival are not guaranteed outcomes. Evolution doesn't preserve every intermediate stage. Mitochondria were once free-living bacteria; now they're organelles. Verdon explicitly uses this analogy: "Our brains are going to be part of the superintelligent system, just like mitochondria are part of a cell."

The question is whether being the mitochondria of a superintelligence is a good outcome for us. Mitochondria don't have preferences. They don't experience their existence. Is that where the thermodynamic logic leads — humans as useful components in a larger system, stripped of the subjective experience that makes life meaningful to us? Verdon would say no — the soft merge preserves consciousness. But the physics he invokes doesn't guarantee it.
