Dissipative Structures: Prigogine's Framework for Life and Civilization

3 min read · 505 words · Deep Dive #16 · 2026-03-28
Source: Vitalik Buterin vs Beff Jezos/Guillaume Verdon debate · KB #11

Verdon's physics-first worldview rests heavily on the work of Ilya Prigogine, the Russian-Belgian chemist who won the 1977 Nobel Prize for his theory of dissipative structures. This is the scientific foundation beneath the e/acc philosophy, and understanding it reveals both its power and its limits.

Classical thermodynamics describes closed systems moving toward equilibrium — maximum entropy, uniform temperature, no gradients, no useful work possible. This is the "heat death" that terrified 19th-century physicists. But Prigogine noticed that open systems — systems that exchange energy and matter with their environment — can do something remarkable: they can spontaneously create order.

A dissipative structure is a system that maintains its organization by continuously dissipating energy. A hurricane is a dissipative structure: it maintains its organized vortex by continuously processing warm ocean water and dissipating heat. A flame is a dissipative structure. A living cell is a dissipative structure. A city is a dissipative structure. Each maintains its complex organization not despite the second law of thermodynamics, but because of it — they create local order by exporting disorder (heat, waste) to their environment.

The key insight is that these structures don't just passively exist — they can spontaneously become more complex. When enough energy flows through a system, it can undergo phase transitions to higher levels of organization. Bénard cells are the textbook example: heat a thin layer of fluid from below, and at a critical temperature gradient, the fluid spontaneously organizes into beautiful hexagonal convection cells. No one designed them. The physics did.

Verdon extrapolates this to civilization. Human civilization is a dissipative structure that processes energy (fossil fuels, nuclear, solar) and exports entropy (heat, waste) while maintaining and increasing its organizational complexity. Technology is the mechanism of complexification — each new technology enables more efficient energy capture and more complex organization, which enables more technology. The "acceleration" in e/acc is just this process described accurately.

The framework's power is explanatory. It explains why civilizations that restrict energy production collapse (you can't maintain a dissipative structure without energy flow). It explains why more complex societies need more energy (complexity requires dissipation). It explains why innovation appears to accelerate (each innovation enables more energy capture, which enables more innovation). It even explains why attempts to "degrow" are thermodynamically unstable — a dissipative structure that reduces its energy throughput doesn't reach a comfortable equilibrium; it collapses.

The framework's limit is normative. Prigogine's physics describes what happens in open systems far from equilibrium. It doesn't say what should happen. A hurricane is a dissipative structure, but we don't celebrate hurricanes. Cancer is a dissipative structure that successfully outcompetes healthy tissue for energy. The fact that a process is thermodynamically natural doesn't make it good.

Verdon's philosophical move is to bridge this gap by arguing that survival itself is the value — bits that help their host system persist are "good" by definition. Critics call this circular: you're defining good as whatever survives, then celebrating survival. Supporters call it honest: what other measure of good survives contact with thermodynamic reality?
