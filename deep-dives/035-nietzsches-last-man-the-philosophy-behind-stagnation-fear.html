Nietzsche's Last Man: The Philosophy Behind Stagnation Fear

3 min read · 580 words · Deep Dive #35 · 2026-03-28
Source: The Techno-Optimist Manifesto — Marc Andreessen · KB #2

Andreessen quotes Friedrich Nietzsche's Last Man at length, identifying it as the philosophical portrait of everything techno-optimism stands against. The passage is from Thus Spoke Zarathustra (1883), and it is Nietzsche at his most prescient — a satirical prophecy of a type of person who had barely emerged in his time but who he could already see coming: the person who has optimized for comfort and safety above all else, who has abolished suffering by abolishing aspiration.

The Last Man's defining trait is not laziness or stupidity — it's the elimination of tension. He has "discovered happiness," Nietzsche writes, with heavy irony. He blinks. Everything that would require him to stretch beyond himself — love, creation, longing, the pursuit of stars — he has domesticated into manageable pleasures. He is healthy, well-fed, and content in a way that makes him, to Nietzsche, the most contemptible figure imaginable. He cannot despise himself, which means he cannot transcend himself.

Nietzsche positions the Last Man as the endpoint of a certain trajectory of liberal modernity — not its failure, but its success. A society that optimizes for equal, universal, low-intensity well-being naturally produces Last Men, because the elimination of hierarchy and suffering also eliminates the conditions for greatness. This is the tension at the heart of Nietzsche's politics, and it's genuinely uncomfortable: his critique of the Last Man is also a critique of democracy's tendency toward mediocracy.

The philosophical stakes matter because Andreessen is using Nietzsche to diagnose a cultural phenomenon, not just a political one. The risk-averse, precautionary, decelerationist worldview he opposes is not simply wrong about cost-benefit calculations — it is, in his framing, a manifestation of the Last Man psychology. It is the blink. The comfortable incomprehension of "what is creation?" The choice to optimize for the absence of bad outcomes rather than the pursuit of good ones.

Ressentiment — Nietzsche's term for the reactive resentment of those who cannot achieve greatness and instead redefine greatness as evil — is the emotional engine of this worldview. The person who cannot build resents the builder. Unable to match the achievement, the ressentiment figure recodes the achievement as dangerous, exploitative, hubristic. This is a psychology, not a policy position. And it produces a kind of ideological capture where technical progress is consistently framed as threat.

The historical context for Zarathustra is worth noting. Nietzsche was writing in the immediate aftermath of European industrialization — the period when it was becoming clear that technology could radically transform material conditions, and the first rumblings of socialist critique were becoming organized. He saw clearly that one response to this transformation would be reactive: to use the language of justice and safety to arrest the transformation and preserve existing hierarchies under a new moral vocabulary. The Last Man is, among other things, a portrait of the reaction to progress rather than the product of it.

The hard part of Nietzsche's argument is separating it from its later misappropriations. The Nazis read Nietzsche's "Übermensch" as biological and racial — a catastrophic misreading that Nietzsche explicitly anticipated and condemned. The Übermensch is a figure of self-overcoming, not domination of others. What it demands is the capacity to set one's own values, to create meaning rather than inherit it, to aspire without guarantees. This is the spirit Andreessen is invoking against stagnation — not a call for hierarchy, but a call for ambition. The question the Last Man can never answer, because he's stopped asking it: what are you building toward?
