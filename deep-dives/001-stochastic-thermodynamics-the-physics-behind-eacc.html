Stochastic Thermodynamics: The Physics Behind E/ACC

2 min read · 459 words · Deep Dive #1

The intellectual backbone of Beff Jezos' entire worldview isn't philosophy — it's physics. Specifically, a field called stochastic thermodynamics, which studies systems far from equilibrium. To understand e/acc, you have to understand this framework, because everything else flows from it.

Classical thermodynamics tells us that closed systems tend toward maximum entropy — disorder. But life does the opposite. Organisms create local pockets of order by consuming energy and dissipating heat into their environment. A cell, a city, a civilization — they're all dissipative structures, a concept introduced by Nobel laureate Ilya Prigogine in the 1970s. These structures maintain themselves by continuously processing energy flows, and they tend to become more complex over time.

Stochastic thermodynamics formalizes this at the microscopic level. It describes how individual molecular machines — like the proteins in your cells — operate under thermal fluctuations. The key insight is that these systems aren't just passively decaying; they're actively harvesting energy from their environment to do useful work. The math shows that there's a deep connection between information processing and thermodynamic work: every bit of information a system uses to predict its environment can be converted into useful work (this is related to Landauer's principle and Maxwell's demon thought experiments).

Verdon extends this framework to civilizational scale. If biological systems that better predict their environment and capture more energy are selected for, then the same logic applies to technologies, companies, cultures, and entire civilizations. Those that "accelerate" — that increase their rate of energy capture and information processing — persist. Those that don't are outcompeted and eventually disappear. This is why he says acceleration is "like gravity" — it's not a choice or ideology, it's a selection pressure baked into the physics.

The controversial leap is going from "this is what physics describes" to "this is what we should optimize for." Critics argue this is a naturalistic fallacy — just because evolution selects for growth doesn't mean growth is morally good. Verdon would counter that moral frameworks that ignore thermodynamic reality are self-defeating: cultures that adopt degrowth mindsets are literally reducing their fitness and accelerating their own extinction.

What makes this framework powerful is its predictive capacity. It explains why civilizations that restrict energy production decline, why open systems outperform closed ones, and why information technologies (which reduce friction in energy/resource allocation) consistently produce the largest leaps in human flourishing. It also predicts that artificial intelligence — as a meta-technology that amplifies prediction and optimization — should be the single most important technology in human history. Which is exactly what we're seeing.

Whether you buy the full philosophical package or not, the physics is real. And understanding it is essential to engaging seriously with the acceleration debate, rather than treating it as mere tech bro optimism.
