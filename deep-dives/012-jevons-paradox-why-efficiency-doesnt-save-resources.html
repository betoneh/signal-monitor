Jevons' Paradox: Why Efficiency Doesn't Save Resources

3 min read · 504 words · Deep Dive #12 · 2026-03-28
aicomputingeacceconomicsenergyinformation_theorynuclearphysics

Verdon invokes Jevons' paradox as a key mechanism in his vision of civilizational growth. It's a 160-year-old economic observation that remains one of the most counterintuitive and important ideas in understanding technology's trajectory.

In 1865, English economist William Stanley Jevons observed something strange about coal consumption in Britain. James Watt's steam engine had dramatically improved the efficiency of coal use — you could get much more work from the same amount of coal. The logical expectation was that Britain would use less coal. The opposite happened. Coal consumption skyrocketed. Why? Because improved efficiency made coal-powered activities cheaper, which made them economically viable in vastly more applications, which increased total demand far beyond what efficiency gains had saved.

This pattern recurs across every technology domain. More fuel-efficient cars didn't reduce gasoline consumption — they made driving cheaper, so people drove more and bought more cars. More efficient lighting didn't reduce electricity for illumination — it made light so cheap that we now illuminate everything, everywhere, 24 hours a day. The total energy spent on lighting has increased by orders of magnitude since the incandescent bulb.

Verdon applies this directly to AI computing. If Extropic delivers 10,000x more intelligence per watt, the naive prediction is that AI will use 10,000x less energy. Jevons says the opposite: AI will use vastly more energy, because intelligence will become so cheap that it gets embedded into everything. Every device, every process, every decision will be AI-augmented. The total demand for intelligence (and thus energy) will explode.

This is the mechanism that pushes civilizations up the Kardashev scale. Each efficiency improvement doesn't reduce resource consumption — it unlocks new categories of consumption that were previously uneconomical. Nuclear fusion won't mean we use less energy; it'll mean we use so much energy that we terraform Mars and build orbital habitats. This is the thermodynamic ratchet: efficiency improvements are irreversible expansions of the possible.

The paradox also has implications for environmental policy. If you believe Jevons (and the empirical evidence is overwhelming), then efficiency mandates alone cannot reduce total resource consumption. They can only shift what resources are consumed for. Making solar panels cheaper doesn't reduce energy use — it makes energy so cheap that we use more of it. This isn't an argument against efficiency or renewables; it's an argument that efficiency is an accelerant, not a brake.

For the AI debate specifically, Jevons' paradox undermines the "we can control AI by controlling compute" argument. Making compute scarce doesn't prevent AI development — it redirects it toward more efficient architectures (exactly what Extropic is building), which eventually produce more total intelligence than the unrestricted path would have. Hardware restrictions might buy time, but they also incentivize the innovations that make restrictions irrelevant.

The deepest implication is civilizational: there is no steady state. Efficiency gains always drive expansion. The only question is how fast and in what direction. This is why Verdon frames deceleration as thermodynamically impossible — you can redirect the flow, but you cannot stop it without the system collapsing.
