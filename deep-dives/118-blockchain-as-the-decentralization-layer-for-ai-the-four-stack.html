Blockchain as the Decentralization Layer for AI: The Four-Stack

3 min read · 640 words · Deep Dive #118 · 2026-04-11
Source: On Tech Politics/Policy — Marc Andreessen & Ben Horowitz (a16z, April 2024) · KB #15

One of the most concrete frameworks Andreessen and Horowitz lay out is what they call the four layers of AI that need decentralization, and their argument that blockchain is the right tool for all four. This isn't a vibes-based crypto pitch. It's a structural answer to the centralization problem AI is currently barreling into.

Layer 1: Training. State-of-the-art models cost $100M+ to train today, and the consensus number for next-generation models is north of $1 billion. That economics excludes everyone except the top three companies and the governments willing to subsidize them. Decentralized training — where compute is contributed by many participants and coordinated via on-chain incentives — is slower and harder, but it's the only way to put model creation in the hands of academia, startups, and open-source communities. Projects like Prime Intellect and Nous Research are already proving the architecture works. The blockchain piece isn't the model — it's the coordination and payment layer that lets thousands of independent compute providers cooperate without trusting each other.

Layer 2: Inference. Once a model exists, running it for users is the larger long-term cost. Decentralized inference networks (Akash, io.net, Bittensor's subnet 4) let GPU owners offer compute to a permissionless market. Customers get redundancy and price competition; providers get utilization on otherwise idle hardware. The blockchain provides the trustless settlement and reputation primitives that make a decentralized GPU market actually function. In a centralized world, you have to trust AWS or Microsoft. In the decentralized world, you trust the protocol — and the protocol is enforced by code.

Layer 3: Data marketplaces. This is the layer that solves both the consent problem and the bias problem at once. Right now, AI training data is acquired through aggressive scraping — companies hoover up everything on the internet, train on it, and sometimes get sued. Horowitz's framing is sharper: the people who created the data should have a say in whether it's used, and a way to be paid if it is. Blockchain enables permissioned data sharing, automated payments per training run, and an audit trail of what went into a model. The result is a healthier economic relationship between creators and AI builders, and a transparency layer that lets you actually answer the question "what was this model trained on?"

Layer 4: Provenance and deepfake defense. This is the most underrated of the four. Andreessen says deepfakes are the #1 AI issue politicians are personally focused on — every congressperson has a nightmare scenario of a fabricated video dropping three days before an election. The standard response (build AI detectors that scan for AI-generated content) is doomed: detection is a losing arms race because generators improve faster than scanners. The blockchain alternative is inversion: instead of trying to prove something is fake, prove what's real. Cryptographic identity, content signing via public-key infrastructure, and on-chain provenance let humans certify their own content. Suddenly, the burden of proof flips. Unsigned content is treated as suspect by default. Signed content traces back to a verifiable source.

The thing that ties all four layers together is the same political reality: the SEC's ongoing harassment campaign against the crypto industry is actively preventing the AI decentralization stack from being built in the United States. Every regulatory attack on a DeFi protocol is also an attack on the infrastructure that would let a research university train a model, or a creator get paid for their data, or a politician have a defense against a deepfake. The two policy domains aren't separate. They're the same fight, viewed from different angles.

If you want decentralized AI, you want clear and supportive crypto regulation. If you want crypto to matter, you want to be building the infrastructure that AI will need. The two are converging — and the people lobbying against one are functionally lobbying against the other.
