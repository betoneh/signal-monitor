Today's Deep Dive: The Nine Waves of Silicon Valley: From Chips to AI Without a Master Plan

610w
Source: Marc Andreessen: The Real AI Boom Hasn't Even Started Yet — Lenny's Podcast · KB #5

History, when it is actually happening, does not feel like history. It feels like a series of locally rational decisions made by people who mostly do not understand what they are doing at the scale that matters. This is the central paradox of Silicon Valley's seventy-year run: one of the most consequential technological sequences in human history was assembled without a master plan, by an ecosystem that, wave by wave, repeatedly surprised itself.

Marc Andreessen, in conversation with Lenny Rachitsky, maps the sequence from memory — and the list is worth sitting with. Semiconductors. Personal computers. Workstations. Client-server computing. The internet. Mobile. Social. Cloud. And now AI. Nine distinct waves, each with its own cast of founders, investors, and dominant companies. Each wave seemed, from inside, like the culmination of what came before. None of them was. Each was a platform for the next.

The semiconductor wave — anchored by Fairchild Semiconductor and then Intel — was, in the 1960s, primarily a defense story. Integrated circuits reduced the weight of guidance systems in missiles. The civilian applications were an afterthought. The personal computer wave, launched by Apple and MITS in the mid-1970s, was not a natural consequence of the semiconductor wave — it required a specific additional insight, that ordinary people might want to own and operate a computer, which was genuinely non-obvious at the time and widely derided inside the industry. The internet wave, sparked in large part by Andreessen's own Mosaic browser in 1993, was similarly not a predictable extension of the PC wave. It required the additional insight that computers connected to each other were qualitatively different from computers that were not.

What makes the nine-wave model useful is not that it retroactively organizes the chaos into a tidy sequence. It is that it reveals a structural pattern without a central organizer. Each wave creates the infrastructure for the next. Semiconductors enabled PCs. PCs enabled the internet. The internet enabled mobile. Mobile generated the data and user behavior that made cloud economics viable. Cloud economics lowered the barrier to training large neural networks. Large neural networks became AI. But no one in the semiconductor labs of the 1960s was working toward AI. The destination was not visible from any individual node in the system.

The implication for the current moment is that AI is simultaneously a destination and a platform. It is the ninth wave arriving — but it is also the substrate on which a tenth and eleventh wave will be built, by people who probably cannot yet name what those waves are. The mistake is to assume that because we are now living inside an AI wave, we are somehow at the end of the sequence. Every wave has felt that way from the inside. The internet felt like the final abstraction. The smartphone felt like the final form factor. Each time, the next wave arrived from a direction that the previous wave had made possible but had not made obvious.

Andreessen's argument is not that the AI wave is uniquely important — though he believes it is. His argument is structural: the pace of wave formation has not slowed, and possibly has accelerated. The time between cloud and AI was shorter than the time between PC and internet. If the pattern holds, the tenth wave is closer than we think, and it will be built on infrastructure that is currently being laid by people who think they are building AI products.
