A persistent puzzle of the first Trump administration was the gap between its stated ambitions and its actual accomplishments. The standard explanations — incompetence, chaos, institutional resistance — captured part of the story. But Peter Thiel offers a sharper diagnosis: the first term was systematically sabotaged from within, to a degree that made meaningful reform nearly impossible regardless of what the president wanted.

This framing recast the second term not as a sequel but as a genuine first attempt at governance. Unburdened by the internal opposition that blocked initiatives at every level — from cabinet officials slow-walking directives to staff leaking to journalists — the current administration has the operational freedom that was absent the first time around. Thiel describes this as the source of his greatest optimism in a long time, comparing the moment favorably to early Reagan as a window for structural change.

The exhaustion of the opposition reinforces the opportunity. After years of maximum-intensity resistance — impeachments, investigations, media campaigns, regulatory weaponization — the institutional left appears to have depleted its arsenal without achieving its strategic objectives. There are no new ideas coming from that direction, only the reflexive repetition of tactics that have lost their potency. This creates space for policy moves that would have been impossible even four years ago.

The historical pattern works against second terms: presidents typically accomplish less, not more, in their final four years. But this administration inverts the pattern because the first term was so constrained. The combination of experienced personnel, an opposition in disarray, and a public increasingly receptive to dramatic reform — especially after the DOGE revelations about government dysfunction — creates conditions that are genuinely unusual in modern American politics.

Whether this window produces lasting change depends on execution and battle selection. The budget deficit alone — running at five to six percent of GDP — represents a structural crisis that has no painless solution. But the recognition that the federal workforce was already largely non-contributing, that NGO funding flows were riddled with fraud, and that procurement systems were designed to prevent competition rather than ensure quality gives reformers specific targets rather than vague aspirations. The question is whether the political will lasts long enough to push through the inevitable resistance.
