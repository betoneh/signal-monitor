Today's Deep Dive: Why AI Can't Choose What Matters — And Never Will

436w
Source: Heaven and Earth – John Ennis · KB #31

The most powerful AI systems in the world share a fundamental limitation that no amount of scaling will fix: they cannot decide what to care about. They can optimize any objective function you hand them — maximize revenue, minimize cost, boost engagement — with superhuman precision. But the choice of which objective to pursue in the first place is not a computational problem. It is a human one, rooted in values that emerge from living a life in a body that feels things.

Follow any goal back to its origin and you eventually arrive at a place where logic runs out. Why does this project matter? Because it serves a larger goal. Why does that goal matter? Because of something above it. Eventually the chain terminates not in a derivation but in a feeling: you care about your children's future, you want your work to mean something, you have a gut sense that one path is right and another is wrong. These root-level values don't come from data. They come from embodied experience — loss, love, fear, hope — accumulated over a lifetime.

Neuroscientist Antonio Damasio demonstrated this decades ago in clinical research. Patients with damage to the emotional centers of their brains could reason flawlessly in the abstract — listing pros and cons, analyzing tradeoffs — but they could not choose. Decision-making requires caring, and caring is a feeling, not a computation. Emotion isn't noise to be filtered from the signal; it is the mechanism by which evolution solved the objective function problem over hundreds of millions of years.

This reframes the relationship between humans and AI entirely. The machine handles the middle of the workflow — production, pattern matching, optimization. The human handles both ends: deciding what to work on and judging whether the result is actually good. Both tasks require a sense of what matters, and that sense is earned through lived experience, not trained on a dataset. The AI industry's implicit assumption that intelligence equals computation leads to the conclusion that enough computation can solve everything. But choosing what to solve is not itself solvable by computation.

This is not a sentimental argument — it is a structural one. Every AI workflow, no matter how sophisticated, is a loop: prompt, predict, evaluate, refine. The evaluation step — is this good? does this matter? should we keep going? — depends on human judgment that is irreducibly subjective. The machine is the most powerful optimizer ever built, but an optimizer without a chosen objective is just an engine idling in a parking lot, waiting for someone who cares to point it somewhere.
