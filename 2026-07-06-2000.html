Today's Deep Dive: DOGE's Real Weapon: Five Years of Remote Work Proved the Government Was Already Hollow

369w
Source: Tariffs, DOGE & Fixing America: Peter Thiel's Skeptic Solutions · KB #33

The conventional wisdom about government reform, as Peter Thiel frames it, was best captured by Milton Friedman: no government program has ever been successfully defunded once started. It's a one-way ratchet. Departments grow, budgets expand, and reform efforts die against entrenched bureaucratic interests. Thiel believes this dynamic may have finally reached a breaking point — not because of political will alone, but because of an accidental natural experiment.

Five years of remote work during and after COVID revealed something that reformers had long suspected but couldn't prove: a large percentage of the federal workforce was already not contributing meaningfully. The private sector, where companies like those Thiel invests in had direct visibility into productivity metrics, discovered that remote work often didn't work. The federal government, with even less accountability, experienced the same dysfunction at greater scale — but with no consequences. Ninety percent of the federal workforce was remote, and nothing broke.

This created what Thiel describes as a preference cascade analogous to Silicon Valley's political shift. Before COVID, skepticism about government productivity was a fringe position. After five years of visible evidence, the public is ready for dramatic cuts. The political cover now exists for reforms that would have been impossible a decade ago — not because the waste is new, but because the awareness of it has crossed a critical threshold.

Beyond headcount, Thiel points to systematic fraud in the NGO and nonprofit ecosystem that receives government funding. He describes a scheme where program descriptions were falsified at multiple summary levels — where money ostensibly going to feed people in Africa was actually funding political operations. He argues these falsifications could be prosecuted under the same fraud statutes used against Oliver North in the Iran-Contra scandal, potentially implicating thousands of government officials. The combination of visible non-productivity and provable fraud gives DOGE unprecedented leverage that Thiel refuses to cap at half a trillion dollars in savings.

Thiel even raises a provocatively optimistic scenario: government workers replaced by AI — or perhaps AI serves merely as the justification, since many positions could be eliminated without any replacement at all. The automation narrative provides political cover for cuts that are really about acknowledging what remote work already demonstrated.
