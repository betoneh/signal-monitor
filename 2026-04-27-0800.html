Today's Deep Dive: Defense-Favoring Geography: Why Switzerland and Islands Produce Better Governance

610w
Source: My Techno-Optimism — Vitalik Buterin · KB #14

Why is Switzerland the world's most durable liberal democracy? The standard answers involve clever institutional design — federalism, referendums, power-sharing — but those institutions didn't arise in a vacuum. Switzerland survived centuries of external pressure, including being surrounded by major powers during both World Wars, because of a deeply defense-favoring physical environment. Its mountainous terrain made invasion catastrophically expensive relative to any plausible gains. The Alps didn't just protect Switzerland geographically; they created the conditions under which genuinely decentralized and democratic governance could survive without being absorbed by a more centralized, more militarized neighbor.

Vitalik Buterin uses this example to make a broader point in his d/acc manifesto: the balance between offense and defense in a given environment shapes what kinds of political systems can survive in it. The anarchist societies of Zomia — highland communities across Southeast Asia that maintained political independence from lowland states for centuries — did so primarily through the natural defense of mountainous terrain. Conversely, the Eurasian steppes, which offer essentially no defensive advantage, produced some of history's most militarized and expansionist empires. The geography didn't determine the political culture directly, but it selected which political cultures could persist.

Historian Sarah Paine's work on maritime versus continental powers makes a related argument focusing on water: island and peninsula states historically enjoyed a natural defensive advantage that allowed more open, trade-oriented governance to develop. Britain, Japan, Singapore — these are not coincidentally the places where market institutions and rule of law are strongest. The combination of easy voluntary trade (maritime access) and difficult involuntary invasion (water barriers) turns out to be optimal for human flourishing. Continental powers with no natural defensive borders face constant existential pressure that tends to centralize power and militarize society.

The political philosophy implication is important: freedom is not just about values, it's about structural conditions. You can believe in liberal democracy all you want, but if you exist in an environment where attack is cheap and defense is expensive, you will eventually be absorbed or transformed by more aggressive neighbors. The medieval Italian city-states had sophisticated republican institutions and vibrant civic cultures — and many were eventually conquered by external powers whose territorial advantages made them militarily dominant. Ideals require favorable conditions to persist.

Buterin's d/acc insight is that we should try to engineer defense-favoring conditions into the digital world the same way mountains and water historically created defense-favoring conditions in the physical world. Encryption is a digital mountain range: it makes attacking individual users' data catastrophically expensive relative to the gains. Decentralized infrastructure is a digital island: it removes the single chokepoint that an attacker could seize to control everything. Zero-knowledge proofs are a kind of political mountain pass — allowing communities to verify membership and trust without exposing themselves to surveillance.

This reframing matters for how we think about technology investment priorities. The question isn't just "does this technology work?" but "does this technology tilt the world toward conditions where freedom-respecting governance can survive?" Switzerland didn't have to choose to be mountainous, but we can choose what kind of digital terrain we build. The engineering choices we make now — whether to build on centralized platforms or decentralized protocols, whether to prioritize surveillance capability or encryption capability, whether to build open standards or proprietary systems — are the choices that will shape what political systems can survive in the 21st century, in the same way geography shaped which systems survived in the 19th.
