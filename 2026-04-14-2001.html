Today's Deep Dive: The Scalded Stove Problem: Why Learning From Mistakes Can Destroy You

495w
Source: Marc Andreessen: Will a16z Go Public & Why Labour Displacement with AI is Wrong (20VC) · KB #13

Andreessen opens with a counterintuitive claim that attacks one of business's most sacred cows: learning from your mistakes can be actively harmful. He calls it the "scalded stove" phenomenon — touch a hot stove once, and you'll never touch it again. Sounds rational. But in venture capital, it's a career-killing cognitive bias.

The mechanism is simple. A VC invests in a healthcare startup. It fails. The VC now has an emotional scar — "healthcare doesn't work." Next year, a different healthcare startup comes along that's genuinely brilliant, and the VC passes because the pattern-match triggers pain, not excitement. The stove was hot last time; therefore all stoves are always hot. The VC feels responsible and disciplined. They've "learned from their mistake." And they just missed a billion-dollar company.

This is particularly pernicious in venture because the asset class is defined by power laws. One investment can return the entire fund. Missing that one investment — the mistake of omission — is infinitely more costly than the $10 million you lost on the mistake of commission. But human psychology is wired to avoid loss, not to seek gain. We feel the pain of losing $10 million far more acutely than we feel the absence of gaining $10 billion. Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory explains this perfectly: losses loom larger than gains, typically by a factor of 2-2.5x. In venture, the asymmetry is more like 1000x.

Andreessen uses AI itself as the perfect example. AI was "a tremendously good way to lose a lot of money in venture capital from 1945 to 2017." Every decade saw an investment boom that crashed — the expert systems bubble of the '80s, the narrow AI promises of the '90s and 2000s. By 2017, every experienced VC had been scalded by AI. The rational lesson was "AI doesn't work." The correct lesson was "AI hasn't worked yet, but the fundamentals keep improving." The VCs who could override their scalded-stove instinct and invest in OpenAI, Anthropic, and the transformer revolution made the defining investments of the era.

The broader philosophical point extends well beyond venture. Andreessen quotes the marriage observation — "the triumph of hope over experience" — and suggests that in many domains of life, you want hope to triumph. The person who's been hurt in relationships and vows never to be vulnerable again is "learning from their mistakes" in a way that guarantees loneliness. The founder who failed in e-commerce and refuses to touch it again misses Shopify.

The antidote, as Andreessen describes it, is institutional: at a16z, he and Ben Horowitz explicitly remind partners to let go of historical emotional reactions. "You no longer have to pay for that sin. You're completely liberated to focus on the opportunity in front of you." It's not that mistakes don't contain information — it's that the emotional residue of mistakes contains more noise than signal, and the noise consistently biases you toward inaction at precisely the moments when action is most valuable.
