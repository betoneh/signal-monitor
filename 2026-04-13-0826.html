Today's Deep Dive: The Infinite Wants Problem: Why Human Demand Never Saturates

622w
Source: The Lump of Labor Fallacy — Marc Andreessen (X thread) · KB #12

The entire edifice of the lump-of-labor fallacy rests on a hidden assumption that is almost never stated explicitly: that human wants are finite and will eventually be satisfied. If this were true — if there were some level of material sufficiency beyond which additional production created no additional demand — then the fallacy would be correct. Eventually, the machines would produce enough, humans would have enough, and there would be no new work to create. The engine would stop. But this assumption is not just empirically false. It runs against the deepest findings of economics, psychology, and anthropology about how human desire works.

The economic case starts with Maslow's hierarchy — not as a precise psychological model, but as a rough map of how wants expand as lower-order needs are met. Satisfy hunger, and people want better food. Satisfy basic shelter, and they want more beautiful, spacious, or conveniently located shelter. Satisfy basic safety, and they want status, belonging, esteem, and self-actualization. The categories of want expand as the categories below them are addressed. There is no ceiling because the goods at higher levels — connection, recognition, meaning, beauty, experience — are not satiable in the way a physical appetite is satiable. You can eat until you're full. You cannot status-seek until you're permanently satisfied.

The behavioral economics literature confirms this with uncomfortable precision. The hedonic treadmill — first described by Brickman and Campbell in 1971 — describes the human tendency to adapt rapidly to improvements in circumstances and return to a relatively stable baseline of desire. Win the lottery, and within two years your reported happiness is back to pre-lottery levels. This is usually framed as a limitation on hedonic gain from wealth. But the other face of the hedonic treadmill is that humans are structurally incapable of consuming their way to permanent satisfaction. The wants reset. The engine keeps running.

The historical evidence is decisive on this point. Americans in 1900 could not have wanted smartphones, streaming services, international air travel, or precision medical care — because these things didn't exist. As technology made them available and affordable, humans discovered that they wanted them intensely. The same pattern will hold for whatever categories of goods and services become available at near-zero cost through AGI: humans will consume them, habituate, and discover that they want the next tier of improvement or the adjacent new category. Demand does not saturate because wants are not fixed. They are generated by the interaction of existing desires with new possibilities.

There is a deeper philosophical argument here that goes beyond economics. Human time is absolutely finite — there are only 24 hours in a day, and human lives last roughly 80 years. Even in a world where AGI produces every material good at near-zero cost, human time and attention remain irreducibly scarce. And because human time is scarce, everything that requires human time — other humans' attention, presence, care, connection — becomes more valuable as everything else gets cheaper. The economist Gary Becker's work on the allocation of time shows that as wages rise, the opportunity cost of time rises with them. High-income societies don't consume more leisure because time is expensive. They substitute market goods (restaurants, cleaning services, professional advisors) for self-produced goods — increasing demand for labor in service categories.

The final implication for AGI is counterintuitive. A world where AGI produces information goods, analytical services, and routine cognitive work at near-zero cost is not a world of less human activity. It is a world where the human-intensive remainder — genuine relationship, irreplaceable judgment, physical presence, embodied craft, live experience — commands a massive premium. The infinite wants problem means that as the machine satisfies one category of want at low cost, human desire immediately flows into the adjacent categories that the machine cannot satisfy. The engine of demand never reaches steady state. It just finds new fuel.
