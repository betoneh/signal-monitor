Today's Deep Dive: Safety Theater as Monopoly Capture: The AI Regulation Trojan Horse

540w
Source: On Tech Politics/Policy — Marc Andreessen & Ben Horowitz (a16z, April 2024) · KB #15

The most important sentence in the Andreessen-Horowitz tech policy video is the one most people will skip past: big tech got language banning open source AI into the Biden executive order — under the guise of safety — to protect themselves from competition. That isn't a hypothesis. It's a documented fact, and Ben Horowitz says it on camera with the same matter-of-factness someone would use to describe the weather.

This is the playbook. It works in three steps. First, you arrive in Washington with a doomsday narrative about the technology you yourself are building. Second, you propose "responsible" guardrails that just so happen to require massive compliance teams, expensive licensing, and pre-deployment government review — all things you can afford and your competitors can't. Third, you walk out with a regulatory moat carved into federal policy, marketed to the public as consumer protection.

The thing that makes this so effective is that the surface-level argument is unfalsifiable. Who could possibly be against "safe AI"? Who could oppose "responsible development"? The framing is designed to make objection sound reckless. But Horowitz nails the actual question buried inside the safety frame: safe for whom? Safe for incumbents whose business model is at risk from cheaper, faster, open-source competitors? Sure. Safe for users, researchers, and the broader innovation ecosystem? Almost certainly not.

The historical analogy is the precautionary principle, which is what the Biden EO's open source provisions ultimately rest on. Andreessen points out that the term itself was coined by the German Greens in the 1970s to ban civilian nuclear power — and the result, fifty years later, is a Germany shutting down nuclear plants while funding Russian gas imports during a war in Ukraine. The precautionary principle is the cleanest available framework for converting any technology into a regulated monopoly, because the burden of proof — "prove it can never be misused" — is impossible to meet, which means the only entities who can field the technology are the ones already big enough to absorb the legal risk.

The deeper move is rhetorical. Big tech has discovered that you can launder a monopoly play through the language of public interest. The same companies that built their dominance on the open internet, open standards, and (in many cases) open source software are now lobbying to make the next generation of those things illegal — and they're doing it under banners like "responsible AI," "AI safety," and "frontier model governance." The vocabulary changes. The play doesn't.

What makes Andreessen's framing useful is that he's willing to name names. Microsoft via OpenAI. Amazon via Anthropic. Google directly. Even Vinod Khosla, who Andreessen says he personally watched lobby Congress to ban open source AI on Manhattan Project grounds. The cartel isn't a theory. It's a coalition with names, lobbyists, and a shared business model — and the safety language is the wrapper, not the content.

The counter-move that little tech needs to make is the unglamorous one: show up, repeat the actual technical facts, and refuse to let "responsible" become a synonym for "licensed by the incumbents." Every harmful use of AI is already illegal under existing law. Regulating uses works. Regulating the math itself is what the cartel wants — because banning the math is the only way to ban the competition.
