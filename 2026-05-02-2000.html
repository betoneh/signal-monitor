Amor fati — love of fate. Nietzsche's most personal doctrine, introduced in The Gay Science and woven through everything after: "My formula for greatness in a human being is amor fati: that one wants nothing to be different, not forward, not backward, not in all eternity."

It's not optimism. It's not stoic endurance. It's something stranger and harder: the active embrace of everything that happens, including — especially — the worst of it. The crash, the loss, the war, the bear market. Not because suffering is good but because the person you become through it is the only version worth being.

BTC ETF institutional investors put $767M in over five consecutive days while the Fear & Greed Index sat at extreme fear for 45 straight days. That's amor fati translated into capital allocation. Not hope. Not speculation. A fundamental conviction that the current suffering is part of the path, and the path is worth walking.

Rearden doesn't enjoy his trial. He doesn't enjoy having his metal seized, his reputation attacked, his marriage weaponized against him. But he doesn't wish it away either. Every obstacle clarifies his purpose. Every attack confirms that what he's building matters enough to be feared. The parasites don't persecute the irrelevant. Amor fati for Rearden means: if they're this afraid of my metal, my metal must be extraordinary.

Nietzsche's eternal recurrence is the test: if you had to live this exact life — every loss, every betrayal, every bear market — infinitely, on repeat, forever, would you say yes? The Last Man says no. He wants things to be different, easier, safer. The Übermensch says "again". Not because he's masochistic but because he recognizes that the struggle is the substance, not an obstacle to it.

The BTC-equity decoupling reads differently through this lens. While equities crater on Hormuz disruption and stagflationary data, BTC holds or rises. The conventional explanation is "digital gold" or "macro hedge." The Nietzschean explanation is that BTC holders have already priced in the chaos — not as a risk to manage but as the environment in which Bitcoin was designed to thrive. Satoshi released the whitepaper in 2008, in the wreckage of the financial system. Bitcoin is amor fati in protocol form — a system built for the world as it is, not as we wish it were.

Erik Voorhees buying ETH again after selling 12,886 at $3,324 a year ago is the same energy. He was wrong. He sold. Now he's buying back, plus tokenized gold. No apology, no excuse — just the next move. That's the opposite of the Last Man's blink. It's eyes wide open, looking directly at the loss, and saying: "Now what?"
