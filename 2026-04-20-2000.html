Source: Marc Andreessen: Will a16z Go Public & Why Labour Displacement with AI is Wrong (20VC) · KB #13

Andreessen identifies a16z's biggest regret: passing on Anduril's Series A. Palmer Luckey — whom they knew well from Oculus — was building a defense technology company. The opportunity was obvious. The founders were exceptional. The market was enormous. And a16z got "scared off" by "the politics, the cultural elements of that at the time."

This is the scalded stove problem operating at the categorical level. It wasn't that a16z had lost money in defense tech before. It was that the cultural zeitgeist of Silicon Valley in the late 2010s made defense technology feel radioactive. Tech workers protested Google's Project Maven. The industry consensus was that building technology for the military was morally questionable. And a16z, despite being run by people who knew better, let that consensus override their investment judgment.

The regret was catalytic. "You'll notice that we are now extremely enthusiastic investors in defense tech and in things involving law enforcement, national security, public safety." Andreessen explicitly frames this as a category-level correction — not just "we should have invested in Anduril" but "we should never again let cultural politics prevent us from investing in an entire category."

This story is Andreessen practicing what he preaches about the scalded stove — but in reverse. Instead of a bad experience causing him to avoid a category, a missed opportunity (Anduril) caused him to open a category. The lesson isn't "defense tech is good" or "defense tech is bad." The lesson is: categorical risk aversion driven by cultural sentiment rather than investment analysis is always a mistake.
