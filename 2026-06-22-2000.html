Today's Deep Dive: When Everything Converges, the Institution Is All That's Left

262w
Source: The Next Biggest Moat in AI — Jaya Gupta · KB #30

AI is producing a strange new competitive landscape where application layers collapse into infrastructure, infrastructure companies climb into workflows, and every startup rebrands itself around the same rotating buzzwords: context graph, system of action, organizational world model. A new category gets named, every website absorbs it, and within weeks dozens of companies claim to be the inevitable platform for how work will change. The visible parts of company-building—product surfaces, interfaces, even early velocity—are now cheap to imitate.

What cannot be cheaply imitated is the institution underneath: the specific way a company attracts exceptional people, organizes their ambition, concentrates judgment, distributes authority, and turns work into a compounding system. Products can be copied. Categories can be renamed. Technical advantages can collapse in months. But the human system that compounds judgment over time resists replication because it is built from culture, incentives, and structural choices that took years to cohere.

This reframes the competitive question entirely. The enduring advantage is not what you build but how you organize the people capable of building it. When models improve quickly and interfaces converge, the shape of the company itself—how it hires, who it empowers, what it makes high-status—becomes the durable moat. Everything else is surface.

The implication for founders is severe: no amount of product velocity compensates for a generic organizational form. If your company is structured like every other company, the market will treat it like every other company, regardless of what your pitch deck claims. The strategic question shifts from what category do we own to what kind of institution are we inventing.
