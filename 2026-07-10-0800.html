Source: Tariffs, DOGE & Fixing America: Peter Thiel's Skeptic Solutions · KB #33

Over the past quarter century, one domain has consistently generated new companies, new wealth, and genuine advancement: technology — broadly defined as software, internet, mobile, crypto, and now AI. Everything else — law, medicine, education, consulting, banking — has remained stagnant, sclerotic, or actively regressive. This is not a matter of insufficient talent flowing into those fields; it's that the fields themselves are structurally hostile to progress.

The stagnation is most visible in the world of atoms versus the world of bits. Physical infrastructure, manufacturing processes, energy systems, and healthcare delivery have seen marginal improvements at best over 50 years, while digital technologies have transformed entire categories of human activity. The reasons are overdetermined: excessive regulation, cultural risk aversion, possible exhaustion of low-hanging scientific fruit, and an ambient fear of the apocalyptic potential of certain technologies.

For ambitious young people, the implications are stark. Elite universities have not only failed to adapt to this reality — many haven't even processed that Facebook was created at Harvard two decades ago. The traditional prestige tracks of law, banking, medicine, and consulting still absorb the majority of top graduates, funneling talent into sectors where individual contribution is marginal and systemic reform is nearly impossible.

The prescription is clear but culturally uncomfortable: go into tech. Not necessarily as a founder, but somewhere within the ecosystem where new value creation is structurally possible. The persistent refusal of elite institutions to acknowledge this reality is itself a signal — it suggests the opportunity remains underexploited, even after 25 years of evidence that technology is the only game in town.
